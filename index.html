The Problem

AI doesn't understand
the words it processes.

Every LLM starts with a tokenizer. And most tokenizers are broken in a fundamental way.

01

Tokenizers are statistical guesses

BPE — the tokenizer used by GPT, LLaMA, and most major models — learns to split text by asking one question: what sequences of characters appear together most often in training data? It has no idea what words mean. It doesn't know what a morpheme is. It splits "unbreakable" into "un", "break", "able" when it's lucky — and into completely arbitrary fragments when it isn't.

02

More tokens = more compute = more cost

Every token is a unit of computation. When a tokenizer produces unnecessary tokens — splitting a single meaningful unit into 3 arbitrary fragments — the model has to do 3× the work to reconstruct meaning it never had to lose in the first place. For Sanskrit, which current tokenizers handle especially poorly, this means up to 20× more tokens than necessary for the same content.

03

The model learns on sand

When tokens don't correspond to meaningful units, the model has to learn meaning indirectly — by seeing how fragments cluster statistically across billions of examples. It works, but imperfectly. The model inherits not just information from its training data, but all the historical accidents, cultural biases, and ambiguities of the language it was trained on.

Two recent papers (arXiv 2601.06142, Jan 2026; Srikant et al., IntelliSys 2025) have formally documented this problem for Sanskrit and called for a grammar-aware tokenizer using Pāṇini's rules. Nobody has built one yet.

The Proposal

A tokenizer that needs
no training data.

01

Sanskrit's grammar is a compiler

Pāṇini's Ashtadhyayi — 3,959 rules, ~400 BCE — generates every valid Sanskrit morpheme from ~2,000 roots. Deterministically. No exceptions. The grammar does not describe how Sanskrit speakers talk. It generates what is grammatically valid. It is a formal rule system — not a natural language.

02

Sanskrit is 2× more token-efficient

A January 2026 paper (arXiv 2601.06142) documents that Sanskrit needs roughly half the tokens of English for the same content under unbiased tokenization — and up to 20× fewer for dense commentary. The paper explicitly calls for a grammar-aware, Pāṇinian tokenizer. No one has built one for ML pipelines yet.

→

The Sphoṭa Architecture

Sphoṭa proposes building that tokenizer — using Pāṇini's rules, not statistical learning. Every token in the vocabulary is a real unit of meaning, derived from grammar, not from frequency patterns in training data. Zero training data required for tokenizer construction. Formally complete for classical Sanskrit morphology.

This is Paper 1. The proposal is to benchmark this tokenizer against BPE and SentencePiece on Sanskrit text — and establish the architecture clearly enough that others can extend it. A machine-native encoding for the vocabulary (Binary Matrix Glyphs) is proposed as a future direction in Paper 2.

How It Started

Three years —
one ancient saying to a cutting-edge insight.

July 2023

One line in a class I wasn't supposed to be in

I was at Dayanand Ashram — an Advaita Vedanta program. I wandered into a Sanskrit class I didn't belong to and stayed only a short while. But one line from the teacher stayed with me for years:

"In Sanskrit, every single sound — not even a syllable, even a half syllable — carries meaning."

I had no framework for it yet. But it didn't leave.

Dec 2025

Sanskrit is not a language — it is a formal system

In Tirupati, using AI as a Sanskrit teacher, something became clear that no one had made explicit before: Pāṇini's grammar is not a description of a language — it is a generative system. Rules first. Like a programming language. You don't memorize Sanskrit words. You derive them. The grammar came before the language.

Jan 2025

DeepSeek's speed secret — and what it implied

DeepSeek R1 published results showing significantly faster inference than comparable English models. The reason was unexpected: it was internally translating English prompts into Chinese for processing. Chinese characters carry more information per token — higher density — so the model was doing less computation for the same output.

And then it hit me. From my Advaita classes, from that one line that had stayed with me for two years: Sanskrit has even higher information density than Chinese. Every morpheme is meaningful by rule, not by convention.

Jan 2026

Validated at Auroville Literature Festival

At the Auroville Literature Festival, I was in the audience at a panel featuring Sridhar Vembu — founder of Zoho Corporation. I asked him from the floor: given the realization that Sanskrit is a generative language — one that uses the Maheshvara Sutras at its absolute core — could this serve as the foundation for the next step of AI and LLM research? Especially as Western AI founders are already saying the era of algorithmic 10x leaps is finished?

His answer: that is exactly the approach their Kerala lab is pursuing. The question I had been carrying for three years was already being worked on — independently, by one of India's most serious technologists. That was the signal to stop floating the idea and start formalising it.

The proposal paper

Using Claude Code to rigorously stress-test every claim — adversarial critique, competitive landscape research across 19 papers, fact-checking against verified sources — the idea was narrowed to what could be formally defended. The result is the Sphoṭa Architecture proposal: the first concrete specification for a Pāṇinian morpheme tokenizer as an ML preprocessing pipeline.

The Difference

Most AI research is optimizing the building.
This attempts to restructure the foundation itself.

Nearly every breakthrough in LLMs — better attention, bigger context windows, faster inference — operates at the architecture layer. Sphoṭa attacks a layer below that: the vocabulary itself.

01

The abstraction level: pre-training, pre-architecture

Transformer architecture, RLHF, RAG, quantization — all of these happen after tokenization. The tokenizer is the first decision made, and it is almost never reconsidered. Sphoṭa asks: what if the vocabulary was compiled from grammar instead of extracted from data? This is upstream of every other architectural decision.

02

Not a new model. A new substrate.

GPT-4, Gemini, Claude — extraordinary architectures trained on statistical approximations of language. The Sphoṭa proposal is not to replace these architectures. It is to ask what they could become if the vocabulary they learn from was formally complete rather than statistically inferred. A better foundation for the same house.

→

First, second, and third order effects

First order: A better tokenizer for Sanskrit. 2× fewer tokens, formally complete vocabulary, zero training data needed.

Second order: A grammar-aware substrate for the 23 official Indian languages — most morphologically rich like Sanskrit. Lower compute cost. Better representation without massive corpora. A model that understands word structure the way a grammarian does.

Third order: If the substrate language shapes what representations are easy to learn — an open question with real experimental support in human cognition — then a formally generative substrate may produce models that reason differently about causality, relationship, and meaning. This is testable. The experiment has not been run.

A Founder's Intuition

What I hold beyond
what I can prove.

The technical claim in this paper is narrow. I want it to be narrow — precision is how you earn the right to say something in a field full of noise. But there are questions underneath it I cannot stop thinking about. They are not in the paper. They are not proven. I put them here, under my name.

I

Why Sanskrit and not English or any other language?

Most languages are additive or agglutinative — they borrow, accumulate, and stack. English borrows from Latin, French, German, Arabic, Sanskrit itself. "Hamburger" carries Hamburg. "Algebra" carries Arabic al-jabr. Every word carries its history as statistical residue. A model trained on English absorbs not just information but centuries of power relationships, colonial associations, and cultural noise it cannot fully unlearn.

Sanskrit is generative. Every word is produced from a root plus suffixes according to Pāṇini's rules — like a mathematical formula. The word for "suitable," anukūla, means literally "along the bank." No cultural sediment. No historical accident. The meaning is compositional, not inherited. A model learning from a generative vocabulary inherits less noise at the representational level.

II

Does the substrate language shape how an AI reasons?

In humans, the language you speak influences what distinctions feel natural to make — the Sapir-Whorf effect. Russian speakers who have separate words for light blue and dark blue distinguish those shades faster in experiments. Sanskrit grammar encodes eight grammatical cases — spatial and causal relationships built explicitly into word endings. If a model trained on a Sanskrit substrate develops different internal representations of the same concepts, that is a testable hypothesis. I think it would. I think it is worth testing.

III

Could it produce less biased AI?

I hold this loosely. I do not know the mechanism. But a model learning from a vocabulary where meaning is structural rather than historical may represent concepts differently — perhaps with less of the accumulated bias of a language shaped by the cultures that dominated the internet. This is testable. Run standard bias benchmarks on both models and compare. I do not know what the results would be. But I think the experiment is worth running.

IV

Machines as a different kind of intelligence

We designed AI to communicate through human language — linear, sequential, built for biological eyes. But transformers do not think sequentially. They attend across all positions simultaneously. We are forcing silicon intelligence to start from a format designed for biological cognition. The Binary Matrix Glyph system in Paper 2 asks: what would an encoding look like that was designed for how machines actually compute? Not mysticism. An engineering question with a philosophical premise: respect what machines are, rather than making them approximate what we are.
